I woke up this morning thinking about the Turkish proverb "Parayı veren düdüğü çalar." It is used in English as "Who pays the piper, calls the tune." It's not the exact word to word translation but the adoption of the idea is the same. Whoever pays, decides.
 
**"Parayı veren düdüğü çalar."**

The Turkish proverb comes from a Nasreddin Hodja anecdote where children ask the Hodja to buy them penny whistles at the market, but only one child actually hands over the money. When he returns with a single whistle and the others protest, he says: "He who pays, plays the whistle."

**"Who pays the piper, calls the tune."**

It's commonly assumed the proverb comes from the Pied Piper of Hamelin legend, in which a town hires a piper in pied clothing to get rid of rats. He plays his pipe and lures them to drown in the river. When the townspeople refuse to pay, he returns and leads the children away, never to be seen again.The legend traces back to an historic event in Hamelin, Germany. In June 26th 1284, 130 children were led out of the town by a piper wearing multi colored clothes, and all the children disappeared forever.

However, no resource connects the proverb to the legend. The oldest form of the proverb "who pays the piper, calls the tune" first appears in 1611, with variants like "those that dance must pay the music". Meanwhile the legend did not even reach a wide audience in the English speaking world until 1842 in Robert Browning's poem _The Pied Piper of Hamelin_. For this reason, it is not considered very likely that the "Pied Piper" is related to the origin of the idiom.

In the meantime Nasreddin Hodja's jokes stem from the oral tradition, some anecdotes may have been attributed to him later, while others may date back much further. First records dating between 13th 15th centuries. These anecdotes traveled across silk road and eventually Europe. It's known that the Turkish proverb emerges from the story of his. But no scholar has found a direct link between the Turkish and English proverbs.

## Polygenesis

The same universal truth may have surfaced independently across cultures. This phenomenon is called polygenesis. Both cultures use the same specific image to express the same truth about money and power. Still, doesn't feel like a coincidence.

In between 11th and 13th centuries the medieval commercial revolution was changing the world. Money and merchant power began to shake traditional authority that had come from nobility, kinship or church. In feudal and pre-market societies, power came from land, blood, or divine authority. A king or a noble don't have to "pay" to decide they just decide. A proverb like "whoever pays, decides" doesn't make sense in that world.

In the 13th–14th century, when the effects of the commercial revolution intensified dramatically when both Nasreddin Hodja and the Pied Piper legend emerges. We may say the proverb captures a new social reality that was experienced simultaneously in both cultures.

Nasreddin Hodja's anecdotes traveled the Silk Road into Europe. Hamelin itself sat on major trade routes, the legend might have also traveled. In the 13th and 14th centuries, merchants, stories, and ideas were moving between Anatolia and Central Europe constantly. There is no evidence of a single root, nor inspiration. I have to accept at this point... it's the psychic unity of humankind.
